I needed to research 16,000 people.

By hand — done properly, verifying each person across multiple sources —
that's roughly **three years of full-time work**. I'm doing it solo, on a flat
monthly AI subscription.

Here's the problem. At PICPA, the Pennsylvania Institute of CPAs, we could
see a big opportunity: a majority of the state's licensed CPAs — the missing
62% — weren't members. But the prospect list was just names and license
numbers. No firm, no role, no way to thoughtfully reach anyone.

Commercial data vendors were an option. They were also inaccurate when we
tested them, and they raised exactly the compliance and brand questions a
professional association should be asking.

So I built a four-stage multi-agent AI pipeline instead:

1. **Anchor** each person's identity — and reject the look-alikes
2. **Corroborate** facts across five *independent* public sources
3. **Weigh** the evidence by reliability — not naive voting
4. **Verify** every value against the page it was cited from

Two design choices mattered more than the model:

- **It runs locally.** Sensitive member data never leaves the building.
- **It declines rather than guesses.** Every field carries a source and a
  confidence level. If the evidence isn't there, the field stays empty —
  no false precision.

The result: thousands of records enriched with a **zero-wrong accuracy
audit**. In a head-to-head test against a commercial data tool, the pipeline
scored 7 out of 10 with zero errors; the vendor managed 2–3 out of 10 and was
*confidently wrong*. The output is a segmented, source-cited dataset behind a
seven-figure membership opportunity.

And the cost side: built solo, on a flat ~$200/month AI subscription — no
per-record API metering, no data-vendor contract, no added headcount. A
consumer subscription stood in for what would have been person-years of
manual research.

## The lesson

The win here isn't speed, though the speed is absurd. The win is **accuracy
you can trust enough to act on**.

An enrichment pipeline that guesses confidently is worse than no pipeline —
someone eventually acts on the wrong guess, and then nobody trusts any of it.
Designing for "decline rather than guess," with a source attached to every
claim, is what turns AI output into something a team will actually build a
campaign on.

That's the pattern I keep seeing in applied AI: the technical build is the
easy half. The design choices that earn human trust — provenance, confidence,
restraint — are what make it usable. Built with a behavioral-science lens on
why people join, and a responsible-AI discipline on how the data is gathered.
